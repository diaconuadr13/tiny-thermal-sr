\title{Fully Integer-Quantized Thermal Image Super-Resolution\\
on Low-Cost Microcontrollers}

\begin{abstract}
Low-cost thermal sensors such as the MLX90640 deliver only $32{\times}24$\,px
frames, which limits their use in presence detection and human--machine
interfaces. We show that convolutional super-resolution (SR) can run
\emph{entirely on the microcontroller that reads the sensor}. Five compact SR
networks (1.6\,k--159\,k parameters) are redesigned around a restricted
operator vocabulary (\texttt{CONV\_2D}, \texttt{ADD},
\texttt{DEPTH\_TO\_SPACE}) so that post-training quantization yields
\emph{fully} integer INT8 models (int8 weights, activations and I/O). Full
integer form is a hard requirement of embedded TFLite Micro runtimes, which
reject hybrid graphs at runtime. On a FLIR-derived thermal validation set,
all quantized models surpass bicubic interpolation (30.56\,dB) by
0.2--2.0\,dB, at a quantization penalty of only 0.17--0.64\,dB. We benchmark
all models on two low-cost MCUs: an ESP32 (240\,MHz, no PSRAM) and a
Raspberry Pi Pico (RP2040, 133\,MHz), reporting measured latency
(102\,ms--10.0\,s per frame), tensor-arena memory (19--129\,KB), flash
footprint (5--185\,KB) and estimated energy. The smallest model sustains the
sensor's native 8\,Hz refresh rate on both boards. Its on-device output
matches the PC reference within $\pm$4 int8 LSB, so desktop-side quality
carries over to the device.
\end{abstract}

super-resolution, thermal imaging, quantization, TinyML, microcontrollers,

\section{Introduction}
Far-infrared thermal sensing is increasingly used for occupancy detection,
touchless interfaces, and condition monitoring, because it is illumination
invariant and privacy preserving. The price of a thermal pixel, however,
remains orders of magnitude above that of a visible-light pixel: array
sensors in the sub-10-euro class, such as the Melexis MLX90640
\cite{mlx90640ds}, provide only $32{\times}24$ thermopile pixels. Single-image
super-resolution (SISR) offers a way to trade abundant, cheap compute for
scarce, expensive optics. In the intended deployments, the only compute
available is the microcontroller (MCU) already reading the sensor.

Running SISR on an MCU imposes a constraint that is stricter than the usual
TinyML size budget: the Espressif esp-nn kernels used by TensorFlow Lite
Micro (TFLM) \cite{david2021tflm} on the ESP32 \emph{reject hybrid models at
runtime}: graphs whose weights are int8 but whose activations remain float32
(the default outcome of dynamic-range post-training quantization). A
deployable model must be \emph{fully} integer: int8 weights, int8
activations, and int8 input/output tensors. In our experience this failure
mode is silent until the first on-device \texttt{AllocateTensors()} call,
which motivates designing for full-integer quantization from the start
rather than converting trained models after the fact.

This paper makes two contributions. \emph{First}, we show that five
representative compact SR architectures (ESPCN \cite{shi2016espcn} at three
widths, FSRCNN \cite{dong2016fsrcnn}, and a reduced EDSR \cite{lim2017edsr})
can all be expressed in a three-operator vocabulary (\texttt{CONV\_2D},
\texttt{ADD}, \texttt{DEPTH\_TO\_SPACE}) with int8-friendly substitutions
that cost little accuracy, so that full-integer INT8 conversion succeeds
\emph{by construction}. \emph{Second}, we present a complete cross-board
benchmark of the resulting models on two popular low-cost MCUs, measuring
latency, memory, flash, reconstruction quality, quantization penalty, and
device-vs-host numerical fidelity, and reporting estimated energy per frame.
All results, code, and firmware are reproducible from a single pipeline.

\section{Related Work}
Compact SISR networks emerged with SRCNN \cite{dong2014srcnn}, which maps a
bicubically upsampled image through three convolutions. FSRCNN
\cite{dong2016fsrcnn} operates in low-resolution (LR) space and upsamples
with a learned $9{\times}9$ stride-2 deconvolution; ESPCN \cite{shi2016espcn}
introduced the sub-pixel convolution (\emph{pixel shuffle}), keeping all
computation in LR space. EDSR \cite{lim2017edsr} scaled residual networks to
state-of-the-art quality; we use a strongly reduced variant. Integer-only
inference was formalized by Jacob \emph{et al.} \cite{jacob2018quantization};
TFLM \cite{david2021tflm} brought the int8 runtime to
kilobyte-class devices. Prior thermal SR work targets larger inputs and
GPU-class hardware; to our knowledge this is the first systematic study of
\emph{fully integer} SR at MLX90640-scale resolution ($32{\times}24
\rightarrow 64{\times}48$) with measurements on commodity MCUs.

\section{Full-INT8 SR Networks}
\subsection{INT8-friendly architecture modifications}
All five networks are built from three TFLM operators with mature int8
kernels: \texttt{CONV\_2D} (with fused ReLU), \texttt{ADD}, and
\texttt{DEPTH\_TO\_SPACE}. Table~\ref{tab:mods} summarizes the deviations
from the original architectures.

\begin{table}[t]
\caption{INT8-friendly modifications per architecture.}
\label{tab:mods}
\centering
\footnotesize
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\toprule
Model & Modification vs.\ original \\
\midrule
ESPCN \cite{shi2016espcn} & Tanh $\rightarrow$ ReLU \\
ESPCN-Light / -Micro & --- (width-reduced ESPCN, ReLU) \\
FSRCNN-ds \cite{dong2016fsrcnn} & PReLU $\rightarrow$ ReLU; $9{\times}9$ s2 deconv \\
 & \quad$\rightarrow$ $3{\times}3$ conv + \texttt{depth\_to\_space} \\
EDSR-Tiny \cite{lim2017edsr} & residual scaling $0.1$ folded into \\
 & \quad conv weights at export \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Three substitutions deserve justification. \emph{Tanh~$\rightarrow$~ReLU}
(ESPCN): ReLU fuses into the convolution's int8 requantization step, whereas
Tanh needs a separate lookup-table kernel; using one activation type across
all models also keeps the firmware operator resolver minimal.
\emph{PReLU~$\rightarrow$~ReLU and deconvolution~$\rightarrow$~sub-pixel
head} (FSRCNN-ds): neither PReLU nor \texttt{TRANSPOSE\_CONV} has a
fully supported int8 TFLM path on both target runtimes; replacing the
$9{\times}9$ stride-2 deconvolution with a $3{\times}3$ convolution
producing $s^2$ channels followed by \texttt{depth\_to\_space} preserves the
learned-upsampling role at lower cost. \emph{Residual-scale folding}
(EDSR-Tiny): the $0.1$ scaling applied to each residual branch is a
multiplication by a constant, so after training we multiply the second
convolution's kernel and bias of every block by $0.1$ and remove the scaling
op; the deployed graph is pure \texttt{CONV\_2D}/\texttt{ADD}. For
single-channel output, PyTorch's PixelShuffle and TensorFlow's
\texttt{depth\_to\_space} coincide, so no channel reordering is needed.

\subsection{Training and quantization pipeline}
Models are defined, trained, and exported in one framework
(\emph{train-what-you-flash}): the exact graph that is flashed is the graph
that was trained, which makes hybrid operators impossible by construction
and removes any need for cross-framework weight porting and parity checks.
Training uses Adam ($10^{-3}$, reduce-on-plateau), MAE loss, batch~32,
300~epochs, with the best checkpoint selected by validation PSNR.
Post-training quantization follows the standard full-integer recipe
\cite{jacob2018quantization}: per-channel int8 weights, per-tensor int8
activations calibrated on 200 training LR frames, and int8 input/output
(\texttt{inference\_input\_type} = \texttt{inference\_output\_type} =
int8). Every exported model is verified by walking its flatbuffer: the
operator set must be exactly \{\texttt{CONV\_2D}, \texttt{ADD},
\texttt{DEPTH\_TO\_SPACE}\} and all I/O tensors int8; the export fails
otherwise.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\subsection{Dataset and protocol}
Quantitative evaluation uses grayscale pairs derived from high-resolution
FLIR thermal captures\footnote{FLIR-IISR frames, $1024{\times}768$; see the
released pipeline for provenance.}: each frame is whole-frame resized
(area interpolation) to $64{\times}48$ ground truth and $\times 2$
downsampled to a $32{\times}24$ input, giving 1238 training and 219
validation pairs. Whole-frame resizing, rather than texture crops, keeps the
full-scene field-of-view statistics comparable to a native low-resolution
thermal camera. Native MLX90640 recordings \cite{zhu2021thermal} are used
\emph{qualitatively only} (demo figures and the firmware's embedded test
frame): no ground truth exists at $64{\times}48$ for them, so no PSNR is
reported against pseudo-labels.

\subsection{Hardware platforms}
\emph{ESP32-DevKitC~V4} (dual-core Xtensa LX6 at 240\,MHz, ${\sim}520$\,KB
SRAM, \textbf{no PSRAM}) \cite{esp32ds} runs Espressif's TFLM fork with
esp-nn kernels. The tensor arena is allocated from the internal heap at
runtime; because the heap is fragmented by the runtime itself, the largest
allocatable contiguous block is ${\sim}110$\,KB, and the firmware ladders
the requested arena size downward until allocation succeeds.
\emph{Raspberry Pi Pico} (RP2040, Cortex-M0+ at 133\,MHz, 264\,KB SRAM)
\cite{rp2040ds} runs pico-tflmicro with a static arena. A single sketch per
model targets both boards via conditional compilation and prints
machine-parseable \texttt{CAS:key=value} lines: arena and heap sizes, first
plus ten sampled \texttt{Invoke()} times, and a full int8 output dump that
is compared against the PC TFLite interpreter output embedded at build
time. Energy per frame is \emph{estimated} as datasheet chip-level active
current (50\,mA ESP32, 25\,mA RP2040, at 3.3\,V) times measured latency; no
power meter was used, and the figures exclude board regulators and USB.

\section{Results}
\subsection{Quality and quantization penalty}
Table~\ref{tab:quality} reports validation quality. Every INT8 model beats
the bicubic baseline (30.56\,dB), by margins from $+0.19$\,dB (ESPCN-Micro,
1.6\,k parameters) to $+2.02$\,dB (EDSR-Tiny). The quantization penalty is
0.17--0.64\,dB and is \emph{non-monotonic in model size}: ESPCN-Light loses
0.64\,dB while both the smaller ESPCN-Micro (0.17\,dB) and the larger ESPCN
(0.28\,dB) lose less. The penalty is a per-architecture sensitivity rather
than a calibration artifact: increasing the calibration set from 200 to all
1238 training frames left it unchanged.

Fig.~\ref{fig:flirgt} compares the deployed INT8 models against the ground
truth on two validation frames chosen by fixed rules (no cherry-picking):
the frame at the 25th percentile of bicubic PSNR (a harder scene) and the
frame with the most spatial detail (highest Laplacian variance). The models
recover the silhouette edges and facade structure that bicubic blurs, and
the per-frame PSNR ordering reproduces the validation-mean ranking of
Table~\ref{tab:quality}.

The board measurements yield four observations. \emph{(i)~Real-time SR is feasible:}
ESPCN-Micro runs at 9.8\,FPS on the ESP32 and 7.8\,FPS on the Pico,
matching the MLX90640's typical 8\,Hz refresh rate \cite{mlx90640ds}, at
under 20\,KB of arena. \emph{(ii)~The fit boundary is memory, not flash:}
EDSR-Tiny (185\,KB flash, 129\,KB arena) runs on the Pico's 264\,KB
contiguous SRAM but cannot obtain a large-enough contiguous block on the
ESP32's fragmented internal heap. This is a concrete, measured illustration
that \emph{usable} SRAM on the ESP32 is bounded by its largest free block
(${\sim}110$\,KB), not by the 520\,KB total. \emph{(iii)~The slower, simpler
core is competitive:} despite a $1.8\times$ clock deficit and no vector
extensions, the RP2040 is only 14--26\% slower than the ESP32 on the three
smallest models (widening to 55\% on ESPCN), and its estimated energy per
frame is ${\sim}1.4$--$1.8\times$ lower throughout.
\emph{(iv)~Latency is predictable from the MAC count alone:} across a
$100\times$ span in workload, the effective per-operation cost stays within
a narrow band: 60--84\,ns/MAC on the ESP32 and 83--105\,ns/MAC on the
RP2040. A designer can therefore estimate on-device latency of a candidate
architecture to within ${\sim}40\%$ \emph{before training it}, from the
analytic MAC count of the graph. The most efficient point on the ESP32 is
the full ESPCN (59.6\,ns/MAC): its wide 64-channel convolutions amortize
per-layer overhead best in the esp-nn kernels.

\subsection{Device--host numerical fidelity}
\label{sec:fidelity}
The firmware compares every output pixel against the PC TFLite interpreter
reference. Outputs are not universally bit-exact, but the deviation is
bounded and \emph{identical on both boards}: the ESPCN family differs on at
most 3.9\% of pixels by $\pm 1$ LSB; FSRCNN-ds on 31\% by up to $\pm 4$ LSB;
EDSR-Tiny (Pico) on 49\% by up to $\pm 3$ LSB. Equal mismatch counts on two
unrelated cores indicate deterministic rounding differences in the optimized
int8 kernels rather than platform defects, so PC-side quality metrics
transfer to the device within these sub-quantization-step bounds.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\emph{Choosing a model.} The measurements suggest a simple selection rule.
If the application must keep up with the sensor (8\,Hz), ESPCN-Micro is
currently the only option, and it still clears bicubic by $+0.19$\,dB in
20\,KB of RAM. If frames are processed on demand (e.g., a presence event
triggers one high-quality readout), ESPCN at ${\sim}1$\,s or EDSR-Tiny at
${\sim}10$\,s (Pico) buy $+0.9$\,dB and $+2.0$\,dB respectively. FSRCNN-ds
and ESPCN-Light are dominated in this benchmark: ESPCN reaches higher
quality at comparable or better per-MAC efficiency, and their larger
quantization penalties (0.49--0.64\,dB) erode their float32 advantage.

\emph{Quantization penalty and QAT.} The penalty is post-training
quantization only; quantization-aware training \cite{jacob2018quantization}
would likely recover much of ESPCN-Light's 0.64\,dB, but was not applied
here to keep every model on the identical, reproducible PTQ recipe. The
penalty proved insensitive to calibration-set size (200 vs.\ all 1238
frames). This points to a genuine architecture sensitivity, plausibly the
narrow 16-channel bottleneck concentrating activation ranges, rather than
insufficient calibration statistics.

\emph{Protocol.} The $64{\times}48$ ground truth is itself produced by
downscaling high-resolution FLIR captures, so absolute PSNR values are
specific to this protocol; the supported claims are the \emph{relative}
ranking of models and the quantization deltas. The on-device fidelity check
uses one embedded test frame per model (flash budget), so its mismatch
statistics are not a dataset-wide guarantee.

\emph{Energy.} The energy column is a first-order estimate (datasheet
chip-level active current $\times$ measured latency) that excludes board
regulators, USB, and duty-cycling; it supports \emph{relative} comparison
between models and boards, not absolute power claims.

\emph{Headroom.} Both targets are dual-core, yet TFLM executes
single-threaded, leaving a near-$2\times$ latency reserve that could bring the
mid-size models to sensor rate without touching the models themselves.

\section{Conclusion}
Restricting compact SR networks to a three-operator, int8-native vocabulary
makes full-integer quantization succeed by construction and costs at most
0.64\,dB. The resulting models turn a 32$\times$24 thermopile array into a
64$\times$48 imager entirely on a low-cost MCU: at the small end,
sensor-rate (8\,Hz) super-resolution in 20\,KB of RAM; at the large end, a
+2\,dB model that fits the Pico but is excluded from the ESP32 by heap
fragmentation alone. Beyond the specific numbers, two engineering lessons
generalize: full-integer deployability is an \emph{architectural} property
that should be designed in before training, and on-device latency of this
model class is predictable from the analytic MAC count within tens of
percent. We release the full pipeline (training, quantization with
flatbuffer verification, dual-target firmware, log parsing) so every
number is reproducible. Future work includes quantization-aware training
to recover the ESPCN-Light penalty, dual-core execution, and true power
measurements.
